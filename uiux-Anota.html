Project Overview

Project outline / Clients’ names / Project goals / Major deliverables

Objective:

Improve medical data quality and reduce medical* annotation work time

Overview:

Designing smart interfaces to streamline medical annotation and unlock meaningful healthcare improvements.

Medical annotations help structure knowledge hidden in unstructured text and feed it into machine learning systems. However, many annotation projects struggle with unclear instructions, inefficient processes, and inconsistent quality.

To support Taiwan’s National Health Insurance Administration (NHIA) in creating high-quality medical annotations, our team focused on understanding the real pain points of annotation project managers (primary users) and annotators (secondary users). In most cases, project managers failed to fully describe the project logic or infrastructure. As a result, annotators spent extra time rechecking data that NLP models couldn’t understand or apply.

In the early stage of product design, we surveyed annotation needs—including content, flow, and tool logic. Later, we focused on building an intuitive UI to support non-technical users. Most project managers are unfamiliar with complex software, so we prioritized clarity, ease of use, and minimal learning effort.

Initial feedback confirms that Anota significantly reduces annotation time and improves data quality. The product will be launched online soon.

Starting the Design

Includes problems, solutions, flowcharts, wireframes to get you started

Problem:

Helps project managers develop medical annotation projects and assign tasks to annotators, improving work efficiency by providing a simplified graphical interface

Project managers often provide only annotation files without explaining the project framework, medical terms, or procedures. Tasks are usually assigned verbally, leaving annotators unclear about how to proceed. This lack of context leads to inefficiency and errors.

As such, Anota focuses on mining insights from stakeholders on plans and feedback (questions, concerns, or ideas) for annotation projects. Anota helps annotation teams start projects faster and more efficiently.

Solutions:

Allows managers to upload comprehensive project materials and simply follow the steps on the UI. Anota will have a clear, consistent task assignment for annotators.

Step-By-Step Instructions:

The case manager simply follows the step-by-step instructions to complete the task. Anota will provide a structure that simplifies planning and reduces ambiguity.

Simplified UI:

Anota simplifies its interface by removing unnecessary features and visual effects. Users just need to interact and follow the instructions in the project annotations.

Flowchart:

Managers unfamiliar with digital tools needed fewer steps and clearer structure. They need a logical planning process based on usage scenarios and behaviours. However, the steps should be limited.

Wireframe:

Initially, I suggested putting all the processes and features on one page, but users got confused and it took more time to complete the project. To address this, I went with a one-task-per-page design and added a progress bar to indicate the number of steps left to complete to reduce tension and confusion.

Refining the design

Utilize visual design to help users access and understand user interfaces

UI Design:

The UI was designed to help users complete tasks smoothly while maintaining a simple aesthetic. A progress bar has been added to show users which stage of the process they are in. Use readable fonts and calming blue tones to support user focus in multilingual interfaces.

Going Forward

User Feedback:

Users appreciated how Anota streamlined project management and made the annotation process smoother and more accurate. During COVID-19, remote work highlighted the need for an online version, allowing access anytime, anywhere.

Limitation & Reflection:

Due to contract limitations with NHIA, the team was only allowed to interview and test with a small number of project managers. However, most insights should be provided by the annotators, not their project managers. That is, when the annotator is confronted with the precise problem, we cannot get the full picture of the complete scene. Challenges, questions, feedback, and assumptions from real users (project managers) may still be wrong.
